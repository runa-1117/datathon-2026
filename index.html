Machine Learning

We use machine learning to both predict future air quality risk and identify long-term air quality patterns across U.S. counties. We clustered U.S. counties based on long-term air quality patterns to identify persistent air quality regimes, rather than ranking counties by a single AQI metric.

1

Predicting Future Air Quality

Predict next year's Max AQI or number of Unhealthy Days for a county using historical data.

2

Identifying Pollution Peak Counties

Predict which counties will experience the highest pollution peaks (Max AQI or 90th percentile AQI).

3

Identifying Air Quality Regimes

Cluster counties based on historical air quality patterns to identify high-risk regions.

Method

  • Construct multi-year exposure, baseline, and volatility features
  • Validate feature relationships to avoid redundancy
  • Cluster counties based on long-term air quality behavior

Model Choice

  • K-means chosen for interpretability and stability
  • Elbow & silhouette analysis guide cluster selection
  • K = 4 balances separation and real-world meaning

Counties without sufficient EPA air quality monitoring coverage.

🔍 Finding

By clustering U.S. counties using long-term air quality data, we found that air pollution risk is more complex than a single AQI score. We identified four main air quality patterns. High Burden Mixed areas have the worst overall air quality, often affected by multiple pollution sources and extreme events like wildfires. PM2.5 Chronic counties experience steady, long-term pollution, while Ozone-Dominant areas mainly face seasonal ozone pollution with fewer severe days. Clean but Spiky counties are usually clean but have short pollution spikes from traffic or dust. Gray areas show places with limited EPA monitoring, highlighting gaps in available data.
